Spreadsheets are genuinely enough to run a small cabinet shop until one of two things happens: you quote more than two or three jobs a week, or someone other than you needs to price work. Past that line, dedicated software earns its keep. Before it, save your money and keep your spreadsheet tidy.

## What do spreadsheets do well in a cabinet shop?

I ran my own cabinetry workshop for over 10 years, and for most of that time the whole business lived in spreadsheets. A pricing sheet, a cut list sheet, a job tracker, a tab of supplier prices. So I am not going to pretend spreadsheets are rubbish. They are not.

They are free, or near enough. You already know how to use them. And they are completely flexible. Your pricing sheet can match exactly how you price, with your own formulas for materials, labour and markup, your own columns, your own shortcuts. No software company decided your workflow for you.

A well-built quoting sheet is a real system, not a stopgap. Mine pulled material costs from a lookup table, held labour hours by cabinet type, and had a markup row at the bottom. It priced kitchens for years and it was right far more often than my gut was. If you are currently pricing on the back of an envelope, a spreadsheet is a big step up and it costs nothing. I wrote up the method itself in [how to price cabinet jobs](/blog/how-to-price-cabinet-jobs/), and every part of it can be done in Excel.

Spreadsheets also work offline, they never have an outage, and in twenty years you will still be able to open the file. That is worth something too.

## Where do spreadsheets break down?

Four places, in my experience. Each one on its own is an annoyance. Together, they were the reason my Sunday evenings kept disappearing.

**Re-pricing when rates change.** Sheet material goes up 12%. Now every template and every open quote is priced on the old number. You either update them all by hand or you quietly absorb the increase, and absorbing it is exactly how [I underpriced my work for years](/blog/how-i-stopped-underpricing-my-work/). A spreadsheet has no idea your rates changed. Copies of copies drift, and six months later you find yourself quoting off material prices from two winters ago.

**Cut lists disconnected from quotes.** The quote lives in one file, the cut list in another, and the only link between them is you retyping panel sizes. Change a base unit from 600 wide to 800 during the quote stage and the cut list does not know. That mistake surfaces at the saw, in material you have already paid for.

**Version chaos.** kitchen-quote-v3-FINAL-REVISED.xlsx. Which version did the customer actually agree to? When a job goes sideways, that question matters, and a folder full of save-as copies will not answer it.

**Nothing is linked.** Client details retyped onto every document. Deposits tracked in your head. The schedule on a whiteboard that only makes sense to you. None of it talks to anything else. You are the only thing holding it together, which works right up until you are ill, on holiday, or simply busy.

Task by task, the difference looks like this:

| Task | Spreadsheet | Dedicated tool |
|---|---|---|
| Price a standard cabinet | Copy an old quote, rework the formulas | Pick it from your library, priced from saved rates |
| Update your labour rate | Edit every template and open quote by hand | Change it once, new quotes use the new rate |
| Produce a cut list | Retype every panel into a second sheet | Generated from the same cabinet data as the quote |
| Nest parts on a sheet | Graph paper and guesswork | Nested layout with grain direction and kerf allowed for |
| Revise a quote | Save-as, new filename, hope | One record with one history |
| Customer approval | Print, email, chase by phone | One link where the customer approves and pays a deposit |
| Invoice | Retype the totals into your accounts package | Push the invoice to QuickBooks or Xero in one click |

The right-hand column is not magic. It is the same information entered once and reused, instead of entered five times and reconciled by you at nine o'clock at night.

## What features does a small cabinet shop actually need?

Four things. Be suspicious of anything much longer than this list.

**Quoting that prices itself from your rates.** You should set your labour rate, your markup and your material costs once, and every quote after that should use them. When a rate changes, you change it in one place. This is the single biggest time saver, because quoting is the job you do most often that earns nothing until it converts.

**Cut lists that come from the quote.** The panels should be generated from the same cabinet data the customer approved, not retyped. If the tool can nest those panels onto sheets with grain direction and blade kerf accounted for, better again. I compared the options in [best cut list software](/blog/best-cut-list-software/), including what Americans search for as a cut list optimizer, if that side matters most to you.

**A schedule you can see.** Not project management with dependencies and swim lanes. Just which jobs are in the workshop, which are waiting on materials, and what is promised for when.

**Invoicing and deposits without retyping.** The approved quote should become the invoice, and the customer should be able to pay a deposit by card without you chasing. Card payments have a real cost, which I have broken down honestly on the [payment fees page](/payment-fees), but chasing cheques has a cost too. Mine was about two phone calls per job.

Now the other half, because it matters just as much. Things a small shop does not need: lead scoring, drip email campaigns and sales pipelines built for national installer franchises. Multi-site inventory. User roles and admin seats for a team of one or two. A 3D rendering suite bolted on when you already design in something you like. These features exist because bigger shops pay for them, and they show up in the price and in the menus whether you use them or not. A tool aimed at a five-person shop should feel small.

## What does switching from spreadsheets actually look like?

Here is one quote, before and after, from my own experience.

Before, in my workshop: an enquiry lands. I dig out the closest old quote, save it under a new name, and spend an evening reworking forty rows of formulas. The cut list gets retyped into a second tab. I export a PDF, email it, and wait. The customer rings wanting one less wall unit and a different worktop, so I do a chunk of it again under yet another filename. When they finally say yes, I chase the deposit by phone and retype the whole thing into my accounts package. Hours, spread over a week or two, most of them after dinner.

After, in ProCabinet, the one I built: the client goes in once. Cabinets come from the cabinet builder, and the prices come from My Rates, set once and reused on every quote after. The cut list is generated from those same cabinets, nested onto sheets, and if the job is going to a CNC the layout exports as a DXF that opens in Vectric, Fusion 360 or whatever the machine shop runs. The customer gets one live link in their browser, no login and no app, where they can tick optional items in or out, sign off and pay the deposit by card. The invoice pushes to QuickBooks or Xero. What used to take me hours takes minutes, and I can hand it to someone else without writing a manual first. The full list of what is in each tab is on the [features section](/#features) if you want the detail.

Honest caveat: the saving is not free on day one. You have to enter your rates, your materials and your standard cabinets before the tool can price anything, and that took me the better part of an afternoon. The payback comes on every quote after that. If you quote once a month, that maths is marginal. If you quote every week, it is not close.

I will also say plainly that ProCabinet is a small tool built by one cabinet maker, not a suite backed by a sales team. For some people that is a reason to pick it. For others it will not be, and a tidy spreadsheet remains a perfectly respectable way to run a small shop. The point of this post is where the line sits, not that everyone must cross it.

### Can I keep using Excel alongside it?

Yes, and I still do. Spreadsheets remain the best tool for one-off costing experiments, year-end analysis and anything the software was never designed for. The switch worth making is the quote, cut list, approval and invoice chain, because that is where retyping causes real errors. Quotes and cut lists export as PDFs, nested layouts export as DXF, and invoices push to QuickBooks Online or Xero, so nothing gets trapped.

### What's the cheapest way to start?

The free plan. It costs nothing, needs no card, and after the 14-day Pro trial it keeps every core function with unlimited stock items and 5 saved items in each other library. That is enough to run a real quote side by side with your spreadsheet and see which one gets to a signed approval faster. If the spreadsheet wins for how you work, you have lost nothing.

### Do I need software if I only build a few jobs a year?

Honestly, probably not. If you quote once a month, never hand pricing to anyone else, and your rates rarely change, a well-organised spreadsheet will serve you fine. Software pays for itself through repetition and handoff. Without those, keep your money in the tool fund.
