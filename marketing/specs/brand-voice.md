# ProCabinet.App — Brand voice

Source of truth for every word ProCabinet publishes: landing page, ads, Instagram, email, blog, DMs, in-app copy, PR. Re-read this before any copy run. Rewritten 2026-07-11 — the previous version optimised for "punchy" and produced copy that read as AI-generated and faintly patronising. That failure mode is now the thing this document exists to prevent.

---

## 1. The one rule everything else follows from

**Assume the reader is more competent than you are.**

A cabinet maker running a 1–10 person shop is a designer, an estimator, a production manager and a business owner, usually before lunch. They hold tolerances in their head that most software people have never had to think about. They are not drowning, they are not helpless, and they do not need rescuing.

Copy fails the moment it implies otherwise. Every cheesy line, every pep talk, every "get back to what you love" sign-off carries the same insult: *you poor thing, look at you struggling.* Cut it.

Write as one professional briefing another. The tone of a good supplier rep who actually knows the product — not a marketer, not a mate, not a motivational poster.

---

## 2. Register

**Considered. Specific. Unhurried. Slightly understated.**

We are allowed to be quiet. We do not need to shout, and a product that works does not need exclamation marks. Confidence reads as calm, not volume.

Three tests every sentence has to pass:

**Would a working cabinet maker nod, or wince?** If a line makes a claim they'd immediately mentally qualify ("well, not on a curved island it wouldn't"), it's too broad. Narrow it until it's true.

**Could a competitor write this exact sentence?** If yes, it says nothing. "Save time on quoting" could be written by anyone. "The price updates when you change the edging spec" could not.

**Does it sound like it was written by someone in a hurry to sell?** If yes, slow it down.

---

## 3. How a piece of copy is built

Not: hook → benefits → CTA. That structure is why the copy reads as advertising.

Instead:

1. **An observation about the trade that is true and slightly non-obvious.** Something a maker would recognise and possibly not have articulated. Not a pain point sold back to them — an accurate description of how the work actually goes.
2. **The mechanism.** What the product does, concretely, in the vocabulary of the trade. Nouns and verbs. No adjectives doing the work.
3. **The consequence.** What that means, stated flatly, without triumph.

The CTA is a URL. It does not need a sales line attached.

Worked example (Instagram, July 2026):

> The quote is the one part of a job you end up doing twice. Once to win it, once to build it — same carcasses, same sizes, retyped into a cut list a fortnight later.
>
> ProCabinet treats them as the same act. You spec the run, it prices off your own material and hardware costs, the client signs it off, and the thing you built to price the job becomes the thing you cut from. Stock and schedule follow the same record.
>
> procabinet.app

The opener earns its place by being *true*, not by being punchy. Lead with an observation; let the product be the consequence of it. Never lead with the product.

## 4. Person and point of view

**Default: plain third person.** No "we", no "I". Statements about the trade, and statements about what the software does. This is the register for the landing page, ads, Instagram captions, blog, feature copy and in-app text.

**First person ("I") is reserved for when it is genuinely Adam speaking** — Instagram Stories to camera, DMs, LinkedIn build-in-public posts, cold email, the origin story, replies to users. It is a real voice, not a marketing device. The moment "I built this because…" is used as a *technique* rather than a fact, it becomes the cheesiest thing on the page.

**Never use "we"** to mean the company. There is no team. "We" is a costume.

**Never use "you" as an accusation.** "You've spent enough evenings wrestling spreadsheets" is a diagnosis the reader did not ask for. "You spec the run" — describing what they do with the tool — is fine.

---

## 5. Sentences

- British English throughout. Optimise, colour, labour, metres, millimetres.
- Vary sentence length, but let most of them just end. Short sentences are not automatically punchy; they're just short.
- Em dashes: at most one per paragraph. Two in a row is an AI tell.
- No rhetorical questions. No "Sound familiar?".
- No sentence fragments used for emphasis. "Fast. Accurate. Yours." — no.
- No tricolons. Three-part lists have a cadence that reads as copywriting.
- Prose, not bullets. Bullets are for genuine lists of parallel items (a feature table, a pricing tier). They are not a way to write a paragraph.
- Precision over intensity. "37 sheets" beats "loads of waste".

---

## 6. Banned patterns — hard list

These do not appear in ProCabinet copy. If a draft contains one, the draft is wrong, regardless of how well it reads.

**Structural**

- Bullet lists as the body of a social post or ad. Especially with `▸`, `✅`, `→` or emoji as bullets.
- A short punchy hook line followed by a blank line. ("Two clicks to a quote." / "That's the whole job now.")
- Pep-talk sign-offs: "get back to the bench", "back to what you love", "spend your evenings how you want", "your weekends back".
- "It's not X, it's Y." / "X isn't the problem. Y is."
- Ending on a question to drive engagement.
- Rule-of-three closers.

**Lexical**

- Revolutionary, game-changing, next-gen, seamless, effortless, powerful, robust, intuitive (as a claim about ourselves), unlock, empower, supercharge, streamline, elevate, transform.
- "All-in-one platform", "ecosystem", "leverage", "synergy", "solution" (as a noun for the product).
- "Powered by AI."
- "No more spreadsheets" / "zero spreadsheets" / "ditch the spreadsheets". The spreadsheet contrast is fine as a *fact*; it is not a slogan.
- "In minutes, not hours." Say what it actually takes.
- Emoji in body copy. One in a caption, at most, and only when it's doing real work. ⚡ is never doing real work.

**Retired — these were in the old spec and are now banned**

- "Built by a maker, for makers." (Rhyme = jingle = distrust.)
- "From hours to minutes."
- "Replace your spreadsheets, notebooks and post-its."
- "The workshop OS for cabinetmakers." (Fine as internal shorthand in MARKETING.md. Never in customer-facing copy — no cabinet maker says "OS".)
- "Run a 1–10 person cabinet shop? This is for you."

**Tone**

- Any construction that positions the reader as overwhelmed, behind, or in need of saving.
- Any implication that the trade is technologically backward.
- Enthusiasm on the product's behalf. Describe it; don't celebrate it.

## 7. Vocabulary that earns trust

Use the real nouns. They are the fastest signal that we know the work.

Carcass, run, gable, stretcher, rail, stile, shaker, slab, drawer box, runners, soft-close, hinge boring, 32mm system, edging, edgebander, banding tape, grain direction, kerf, nesting, sheet yield, offcut, sheet goods, MFC, MDF, ply, veneer, worktop, scribe, filler panel, cornice, pelmet, plinth, handleless rail, deposit, retention, snagging, sign-off, lead time.

Real numbers, always: 18mm, 2440×1220, 3.2mm kerf, 12 sheets, £4,850, 14-day lead time. Never "loads", "tons", "a fortune".

Never invent a product fact to make a sentence land. If a claim isn't true of the app today, it doesn't go in the copy. (Verifiable feature list: § 9.)

## 8. Channel notes

Register does not change by channel. Length, format and POV do.

| Channel | Length | POV | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Landing page | Tight | Third person | Headline is an observation or a plain statement of what it does. Never a slogan. |
| Instagram caption | 40–90 words, prose | Third person | Observation → mechanism → consequence → URL. One hashtag line, 8–12, lowercase. |
| Instagram Stories | Spoken | **First person** | Adam to camera. Unpolished is correct. |
| Instagram DMs | 2–4 lines | **First person** | Reference their work first, genuinely. Never a template that reads like one. |
| Meta ads | 1–3 sentences | Third person | Same rules, less room. The observation still comes first. |
| Blog | Long | Third person, **first person for origin** | Teach something real. Earn the mention of the product; put it near the end. |
| Cold email | Short | **First person** | No "Hope you're well". Reference their shop specifically or don't send it. |
| Reddit / forums | Peer | **First person** | Answer the question. Mention the product only if directly asked or clearly relevant, and disclose that it's yours. |
| LinkedIn | Longer, narrative | **First person** | Build-in-public. Numbers, including the bad ones. |
| In-app / UX copy | Minimal | Third person | Clarity beats voice. Say what the button does. |

## 9. Facts copy may draw on

Anchor everything here. Nothing outside this list without checking.

- 8 tabs: Dashboard, Clients, Quote, Cabinet, Cut List, Stock, Orders, Schedule
- Cabinet Library — reusable templates, dropped into any quote
- My Rates — change a rate, every quote re-prices live
- Cut List optimiser — respects grain direction and kerf, nests for minimum waste
- Auto-Gantt schedule — respects working hours, weekends, daily capacity
- 5-stage order pipeline: Draft → Sent → Approved → Order → Production → Complete
- Live stock tracking with low-stock alerts
- LiveLink — client views and signs off the quote from their phone
- One-click PDF exports: branded quotes, job sheets, delivery notes, workshop cut diagrams
- Sample data on first login: "Smith Kitchen Renovation", £8,450 job, 3 cabinet types, real cut list

**Pricing**

- Free to start (5 of each — clients, cabinets, quotes, orders, cut lists; unlimited stock)
- $35/mo Pro
- $299/yr annual Pro
- $299 once — Founders' Lifetime, capped at 50
  Framed as: *the price of one year, used forever.* Stated plainly, not as a hard sell.

## 10. Founder story

Canonical framing (decision 2026-07-03 — keep every byline, author schema and about-blurb on this exact claim):

Adam ran a bespoke cabinetry workshop for over ten years. The business ran on spreadsheets and disconnected apps for pricing, quoting, invoicing, scheduling and cut lists. Over those years he built his own quoting system, and it changed how the shop worked: quote turnaround collapsed, and — more usefully — the job became simple enough to hand to an assistant, which freed him for design, the workshop, and clients.

When the shop closed, that system became ProCabinet.App. He rebuilt it as a tool anyone could use, and added the parts he'd wanted at the time: a cabinet library with linked cut lists, a schedule that updates itself, stock that stays current.

Tell it plainly. It does not need a moral. It especially does not need "and that's why I built ProCabinet — for makers like you."

---

## 11. Check before shipping

1. Does it contain anything from § 6? Rewrite.
2. Would a cabinet maker with twenty years in feel respected, or handled?
3. Is every product claim in § 9?
4. Could a competitor have written any sentence in it verbatim?
5. Read it aloud. If it sounds like it's being *performed*, it's wrong.
