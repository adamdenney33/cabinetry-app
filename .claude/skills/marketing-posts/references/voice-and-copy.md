# Voice & copy

The founder writes the copy. Your job is to sound like him: a working cabinetmaker who built
the tool he wished he had. Plainspoken, personal, confident, and specific. Professional, but
not corporate, and never "AI-sounding."

## The voice in one breath

> Real numbers. Plain words. The problem first, then how the app fixes it. Said the way you'd
> say it to another shop owner across the bench.

## AI tells to avoid (hard rules)

These are the patterns that make copy read as machine-written. Avoid them:

- **No em dashes.** Use a comma or a full stop instead. (The founder actively rewrites every
  `—` into a `,` or `.` — do it pre-emptively.) En dashes for number ranges are fine.
- **No aphorisms / fortune-cookie lines.** Don't end on a neat universal truism.
- **No rule-of-three filler.** "Fast, simple, and powerful" triads are out. Use a real,
  specific list (an actual enumeration of fields/steps) or just two things.
- **No hashtag stuffing.** A curated handful, not the whole bank (see below).
- **No hype words.** "Revolutionary", "seamless", "game-changing", "effortless", "unlock",
  "supercharge", "elevate". Cut them.
- **No vague benefit-speak.** Say the concrete thing ("price a cabinet to the penny", "9.6
  hrs / £1,111"), not "boost your workflow".

## House style

- **British English** (colour, optimise, labour, organise).
- **Real numbers, no rounding-to-hype.** £1,111, 9.6 hrs, 5 of each, £75/hr.
- **Second person, present tense.** "You set your rates once, then it's build, quote, repeat."
- **Lower-case sentence case** in headlines unless a product/proper noun.
- **The free tier, stated honestly:** "Free to start, 5 of each, no card." Full functionality,
  hard cap at 5 saved items per library. No feature gates.
- **Quotes give hours as well as cost** — surface time alongside price wherever quoting comes up.

## Landing-page alignment

Pull feature priorities and phrasing from the landing page. Recurring pillars / phrases:

- "The workshop OS for cabinet makers."
- "Quote, cut, schedule and bill from one place."
- "Set your rates once, and price a full custom kitchen in minutes, not hours."
- "Eight connected tabs. One workshop."
- Per-feature hooks: quote a kitchen in minutes; a cut list connected to your stock; production
  that schedules itself; quote → order → invoice on one pipeline; stock that stays honest.

When in doubt, echo a line that already exists on the landing page rather than inventing one.

## Instagram caption anatomy

1. **Hook (first line).** This is all that shows above the "… more" fold. Make it a question
   or a sharp claim. e.g. "What if quoting was this easy?" / "Price a full custom kitchen in
   minutes, not hours."
2. **Setup line.** One sentence naming the feature and the promise.
3. **Body — a short bulleted walk-through.** Use a middle-dot bullet `·` per line. Each bullet
   is one concrete step/benefit. 3–5 bullets.
4. **Payoff paragraph.** The "why it matters" in plain words. This is where hours-vs-cost,
   delegation, or time-saved lands.
5. **Link line.** `procabinet.app (link in bio).` (Link in bio carries
   `?utm_source=instagram&utm_medium=bio`.)
6. **Engagement question.** A real question a shop owner would answer. e.g. "How long does
   pricing one cabinet take you today?"
7. **Hashtags.** 5 always-on + a rotating handful (see below).

> Note: the founder may ask to drop individual lines (e.g. a "free to start" line). Keep the
> link line unless told otherwise — it's the CTA.

## LinkedIn repost

Same hook, but a longer, first-person founder story. Structure:

- Open with the same hook line.
- A paragraph of lived context ("For years, pricing a custom cabinet meant a spreadsheet, a
  notebook and a fair bit of gut feel…").
- What you built and the one idea behind it.
- How it works, concretely (the real fields, the breakdown, hours + cost).
- The line that mattered most to you (the personal beat — e.g. "it's structured enough to
  delegate").
- Close: "Short 9:16 walkthrough below. Built by a maker, for makers, free to start."
- **Link in the comments**, not the body (LinkedIn suppresses reach on body links).
- Few hashtags: `#cabinetmaking #joinery #smallbusiness #buildinpublic`.

Keep the same no-em-dash / no-triad rules. "Built by a maker, for makers" is the founder's own
line — that one stays.

## On-screen copy (in the reels/slides themselves)

Same voice, fewer words. Headlines are 2 short lines with **one amber word** and an amber full
stop. Sub-lines are one plain sentence. Examples that shipped:

- "What if quoting was this *easy?*" / sub: "Spec the parts, get instant pricing and timings."
- "Set rates once, / *build, quote, repeat.*" (whole second line amber)
- "Quote with *confidence.*" / sub: "Set your rates once, hand off the admin."

`*word*` = amber in the render helpers. Keep on-screen sentences comma/period punctuated, not
em-dashed (the founder has corrected this repeatedly).
