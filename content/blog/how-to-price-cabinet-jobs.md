Price a cabinet job in three parts: materials costed from current supplier prices with a 15 to 20% markup, every workshop hour charged at a rate that covers your overheads, and fitting priced as its own line. Write the method down once and apply it to every job, no exceptions.

I priced by feel for years and it cost me dearly, a story I have told in full in [how I stopped underpricing my work](/blog/how-i-stopped-underpricing-my-work/). This post is the method I use now. It fits on a page, and every part of it works in a spreadsheet or in software.

## Why does guessing at cabinet prices lose money?

Because every guess leans the same way. You guess the hours low, because you picture the build going well. You guess materials from memory, and memory holds last year's prices. You guess the final number under the shadow of what the customer might pay. The errors never lean upward, and they stack.

The quieter cost is that a guess teaches you nothing. When the job comes in short, you cannot see which part was wrong, so nothing improves. A written method turns every finished job into a correction: the hours were out on the wall units, or MFC had moved, and you can point at the line.

The method, in five steps:

1. Work out your true shop hourly rate from your own overheads.
2. Cost materials from current prices, with waste counted.
3. Price labour per cabinet type, from recorded hours.
4. Keep overhead recovery and margin separate and visible.
5. Sanity-check the finished price against the market.

## What should a cabinet maker charge per hour?

Work it out backwards from your own costs, not sideways from what the shop down the road charges. Their rent is not your rent.

Start with a year of overheads, every cost that exists whether or not you sell a single cabinet. Here is my last full list:

| Overhead | Per year |
|---|---|
| Workshop rent | £7,800 |
| Power | £1,900 |
| Insurance | £1,100 |
| Van | £3,600 |
| Machinery, blades and sharpening | £1,600 |
| Software and phone | £900 |
| Sundries, never predictable, always spent | £1,100 |
| **Total** | **£18,000** |

That is £18,000 a year before I earned anything.

Then be honest about billable hours. I was in the workshop 40 hours a week, but only about 25 of them were on paying jobs. The rest went on quoting, suppliers, sweeping up and collections. Call it 1,300 billable hours a year once holidays are counted. £18,000 divided by 1,300 is £13.85 an hour just to open the doors. Add the wage you want to take home, £27,500 in my case, which is another £21.15 an hour. That made my rate £35.

If your number feels uncomfortably high, good. The overheads are real whether you charge for them or not, and every hour billed below that line comes straight out of your wage.

## How do you cost materials for a cabinet job?

From a current supplier price list, never from memory. Prices drift upward quietly and memory does not.

Keep one list of the materials you actually use, with the date you last checked each price, and treat anything older than three months as suspect. Count everything on it: sheet material, bought-in fronts, hinges, runners, handles, banding, screws, glue and finish. The small stuff looks like nothing per item and comes to £90 or more on a kitchen. It goes on the quote.

Count waste while you are at it, because you buy sheets, not the theoretical area of the parts. If the panels add up to thirteen and a half sheets, you will still open 15 once grain direction and the saw kerf have had their say. In the kitchen example below, the theoretical area would put £648 of board on the quote; the 15 sheets actually bought cost £720, and if you quote the smaller number the £72 comes out of your wage.

Where that list lives matters less than the fact that it exists. Mine lived in a spreadsheet tab for years, and [cabinet software or spreadsheets](/blog/cabinet-software-or-spreadsheets/) is my honest take on when each one is enough.

## Should you price labour per cabinet or per job?

Per cabinet type, always. A whole-job guess like "about two weeks" hides everything, which is why it feels comfortable. A per-cabinet number can be checked against the last job, and after a few kitchens it stops being a guess.

Mine settled at 6 hours of workshop time for a standard base unit and 5 for a wall unit, carcass built, fronts hung, hardware fitted. Drawer stacks, corner units and anything with an angle carried their own, higher numbers. Fitting stays separate, priced per day; a day on site has different costs from a day at the bench.

Revisions get painless too: when the customer drops a wall unit, 5 hours and its materials come off the price, no re-guessing. The one rule is that per-cabinet hours come from recorded time on finished jobs, written on a clipboard by the bench, because quoting imagined hours was the single biggest leak in my own pricing.

## What markup should you put on materials?

Between 15 and 20% for a small shop. That is not padding. It covers real costs that sit on top of the invoice price: your time ordering and collecting, the money tied up between paying the merchant and being paid yourself, the board that turns out bowed, the warranty repair two years on. The band matters at both ends: below 10% you are effectively lending customers money for free, and much above 25% the markup is usually compensating for an hourly rate set too low.

The waste you cannot plan for belongs in that margin as well, the miscut panel and the sheet damaged in handling; the predictable kind went into the material line in step two. Both are bigger than most of us admit, and I have put proper numbers on the sheet side in [how to reduce plywood waste with nesting](/blog/reduce-plywood-waste-nesting/), because shrinking the waste is the other half of protecting the margin.

## Why should overheads and margin be separate numbers?

Because when they blur into one, you cannot tell which is failing. Here the overhead recovery lives inside the hourly rate, the £13.85 slice of the £35, and the margin lives in the materials markup. Each has one job. When a job pays worse than quoted, you can ask which line failed: hours running over is an estimating problem, materials outrunning the markup is a buying problem. One blended "add 30% and hope" number can never tell you, so the same mistake gets made twice.

Worth saying plainly: the wage inside the rate is pay for your hands, not profit for the business. For years my quotes carried no profit line beyond that wage, which is partly why the workshop never built a buffer for a dead month or a broken machine. If you want one, add it as its own visible percentage rather than hoping the markup quietly covers it. ## How do you sanity-check a price against the market?

After the sum is done, never instead of it. Hold the finished number against what comparable work goes for near you: kitchens you lost where you later heard the winning price, what local kitchen companies charge for a similar spec, what other makers say over a coffee. You are not looking for a number to copy, only checking that your price sits in the believable range for what you build.

A little above the middle is where a small shop doing decent work should land. Miles above it means your overheads are too heavy for your market or your hours per cabinet are slow. Well below it is not a win; go back through the sheet and find the missing hours.

The check must never reach into the estimate and trim lines. If you move a price to win a job you want, do it as a visible discount on the total, so the quote still shows the true number and what the job cost you to win.

## How do you price a full kitchen, start to finish?

Here is a straightforward example: a 12-cabinet kitchen, 8 base and 4 wall units, 18mm MFC carcasses with sprayed fronts bought in.

| Line | Working | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| Sheet material | 15 sheets of 18mm MFC at £48 | £720 |
| Doors and drawer fronts | bought in, sprayed | £1,150 |
| Hardware | hinges, runners, handles | £340 |
| Edging and consumables | banding, screws, glue | £90 |
| Materials subtotal | | £2,300 |
| Materials markup | 20% of £2,300 | £460 |
| Workshop labour | 68 hours at £35 | £2,380 |
| Fitting | 2 days at £280 | £560 |
| Quote total | | £5,700 |

The labour line is step three doing its work: 8 base units at 6 hours and 4 wall units at 5. It is still the number to distrust. Materials are checkable; hours are where optimism hides, so those per-cabinet figures come from recorded hours on past jobs, not from the build I imagine going perfectly, because the gap between those two was one of the main ways I underpriced for years.

Do that sum in a spreadsheet and it works. The catch is keeping it current: when MFC goes up or your rate changes, every saved template still holds the old numbers. ## Should you show the customer the price breakdown?

Show totals and choices, not workings. One figure for the supplied kitchen, one for fitting, and the options priced as their own lines so the customer can take them or leave them: the larder interior, the second oven housing, the under-cabinet lights.

On your own costing sheet, keep labour and materials separate, because that is how you catch drift in either one. Fold them together only on the customer's copy: an itemised labour line invites haggling over the single number the customer cannot judge fairly.

What I never show is the hourly rate or the markup. Customers compare rates out of context, and they cannot compare your overheads. ## Common questions

### What margin should a cabinet shop make?

There is no official number, and for years my own answer was accidental, whatever was left when the job closed. Pricing deliberately, I aimed for roughly 10% of the job price left after materials, my wage at the full rate and every overhead; some jobs made it, some did not. The figure matters less than margin being decided on the quote rather than discovered afterwards. If nothing is ever left, the fault is usually the rate or the hours, not the markup.

### Should I charge for quoting?

Not for a straightforward kitchen priced from a survey and a conversation; the cost of quoting sits in your overheads, which is why the unpaid hours were counted when the £35 rate was built. Design-heavy work is different. Drawings a customer could carry to another workshop are work product, so I priced the design stage as its own small job and credited it against the build when it went ahead.

### How do I price against big-box cabinets?

Not head-on. A flat-pack range from a national retailer is priced on a volume no workshop will ever have, so matching it means paying for the privilege of working. Price your normal way, then help the customer compare like with like: the big-box figure usually excludes survey, fitting, scribing to walls that are not square, and anyone answering the phone afterwards. Some customers still choose the box, and they were never yours. The ones who ring a maker usually already want what the box cannot do, a kitchen built to their room.

### What if my calculated rate is much higher than what I charge now?

Move to it on new quotes rather than nudging up a few per cent a year while your costs climb faster. Work already agreed stays at the agreed price. Expect to lose a few price shoppers, and expect the sums to work anyway. Fewer jobs at the right rate beats a full book at the wrong one.

### What deposit should I take on a cabinet job?

Enough that the materials are never your risk. I took 40%, and 30 to 50% is the range I hear from other makers. Take it before anything is ordered, make paying it easy, and treat the job as unbooked until the money lands, because until then nothing has actually been agreed except in words.
