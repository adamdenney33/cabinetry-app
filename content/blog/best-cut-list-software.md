## What should cut list software actually do?

Strip the marketing away and the job is short. You give it a list of panels, it fits them onto sheets, and it hands you something the workshop can cut from. Everything else is decoration. Six things decide whether a tool is any good at that job.

| What to check | What good looks like |
|---|---|
| Nesting quality | High yield on a real mixed job, with offcuts big enough to keep |
| Grain direction | Lock doors and drawer fronts to the grain, let hidden parts rotate |
| Kerf allowance | Set your blade width once and forget it |
| DXF or CNC export | The layout leaves the software and reaches the machine |
| Price model | Know whether you are renting or buying |
| Platform | Web runs on anything; desktop in this field means Windows |

Yield is where the real money sits. A few percentage points saved across a year of sheet material pays for any tool on this list several times over, and I have put actual numbers on that in [how to reduce plywood waste with nesting](/blog/reduce-plywood-waste-nesting/).

Kerf matters more than people admit. A 3mm blade across a row of rips does not sound like much until the last panel comes off 12mm short. Grain matters for the parts the customer sees. Any optimiser that cannot do both is a toy, and everything on this list can, so the real differences are price, platform and what happens to the layout after it is drawn.

## Is OptiCutter any good?

OptiCutter is a web app, nothing to install, which suits a shop where the office computer is whatever laptop is nearest. It does both linear cutting for lengths and sheet nesting for panels. The free plan covers 5 stock sizes, 20 part sizes and 500 parts, as of July 2026, which is enough to run a real kitchen through it and judge the layouts yourself. Paid plans are €9 a month for Standard and €19 for Professional, as of July 2026, and every plan, the free one included, exports to PDF, XLS, CSV and DXF. The layouts are decent and the printed plans are clear. My only real gripe is that it is a general cutting tool rather than a cabinet tool, so every panel gets typed in by hand, every time.

## Is CutList Plus fx worth the money?

CutList Plus fx is the old hand of the group. Windows desktop software, one-time purchase, no subscription, which plenty of cabinet makers will find refreshing on its own. As of July 2026 the Silver edition is $89 and caps you at 50 parts per project, Gold is $249 with unlimited parts, and DXF export only arrives in the $499 Platinum edition. It is thorough in an old-fashioned way, with material costing, board footage and purchasing lists built in. It is also Windows only, and the site still lists Windows 7, 8 and 10, so Mac users need a virtual machine and nobody would call it modern. If you want to pay once, own the software outright and cut from printed diagrams, it earns its keep. The $499 step for DXF is the sore point.

## Is MaxCut really free?

MaxCut surprised me. The Community Edition is properly free, with no time limit and no trial countdown, as of July 2026. It is a Windows desktop app built for cabinet work rather than generic cutting, so grain direction, kerf width and sheet trims are all handled without fuss, and it prints cutting diagrams a saw operator can follow without coming back to ask questions. The Business Edition, at $20 a month or $200 a year per device as of July 2026, adds client quotes, labour costs and panel labels, and takes the adverts off the reports. It exports DXF for CNC and beam saws, though not G-code, as of July 2026. If your quoting already lives somewhere else and you want the best free desktop optimiser available, start here.

## What is SmartCUT?

SmartCUT, sold as Smart2DCutting by Rasterweq, is Windows software with pricing from another era, and I mean that kindly. As of July 2026 a single licence is a one-time $85 for jobs up to 100 parts, rising to $295 for unlimited parts, with a year of updates included. The interface looks its age, but the nesting engine is quick and the yield is genuinely good, and it exports DXF, TXT and XML. It also handles edge banding and prints barcode part labels, which tells you it grew up in production shops rather than hobby sheds. If you want a cheap perpetual licence, do not care what the buttons look like, and cut on a panel saw or a DXF-fed CNC, it deserves the trial download.

## Is CutList Optimizer free to use?

CutList Optimizer, at cutlistoptimizer.com, is probably the one you have already used. It runs in the browser, there is an Android app, and the name is exactly what half of America types when searching for a cut list optimizer, which explains how often it comes up. The free tier gives you a limited number of calculations. As of July 2026 the paid Silver and Gold subscriptions remove that limit, and there is a cheap one-off that opens everything for three days, which is fair for a single job. It handles grain direction and edge banding, and the layout exports as a PDF. What I could not find, as of July 2026, is DXF export, so for CNC work the layout stays on paper and you redraw it in your CAM software.

## Where does Cabinet Vision fit?

Cabinet Vision is not really a cut list optimiser, and it would be unfair to review it as one. It is full CAD/CAM for cabinet manufacturing, owned by Hexagon. You draw the job, it generates every part, nests them and drives the CNC directly, with post-processors matched to the machine you actually own. There is no public price list as of July 2026. Pricing is modular and quoted per shop, and the sensible assumption is thousands rather than hundreds. For a production shop with staff and a machine running all day, that money can come straight back in labour and material. For a one or two person shop it is a different conversation, and I have given my view in the questions at the end.

## Which cut list software should you pick?

**You cut a few sheets a month.** Use the free tiers of CutList Optimizer or OptiCutter and pay nothing. At that volume the difference between a 78% and an 82% yield is one offcut, and either tool will stop you cutting a panel against the grain.

**You run a panel saw most days and hate subscriptions.** MaxCut Community Edition first, because free with no time limit is a rare thing. If you outgrow it, SmartCUT at $85 to $295 one-time or CutList Plus fx from $89 gets you a licence you own. Just remember where DXF sits in each price list before you choose an edition.

**Quoting eats your evenings and the cut list is retyped from the quote.** The optimiser was never my bottleneck; pricing was, and retyping panel sizes between files was how mistakes reached the saw. The method I use for pricing is in [how to price cabinet jobs](/blog/how-to-price-cabinet-jobs/), and closing that gap between quote and cut list is exactly what I built ProCabinet.App for. Whether a shop your size needs software at all is a fair question too, and I have tried to answer it straight in [cabinet software or spreadsheets](/blog/cabinet-software-or-spreadsheets/).

**You have staff, drawings to produce and a CNC running all day.** Book a Cabinet Vision demo and compare it against what the machine's maker offers. At that scale the optimiser is one part in a production system, and the quote-based pricing starts to make sense against your throughput.

One last honest note. Every tool on this list will nest better than a pencil and a sheet of graph paper, and the jump from guessing to any optimiser is bigger than the jump between any two tools here. Pick the one that fits how you already work, not the one with the longest feature list.

## Common questions

### Is there a good free cut list optimiser?

Yes, several. MaxCut Community Edition is a genuinely free Windows desktop optimiser with no time limit as of July 2026, and it handles grain direction and kerf properly. In the browser, CutList Optimizer and OptiCutter both have free tiers that cover occasional work, with limits on calculations or part counts rather than on the quality of the nesting.

ProCabinet.App also has a free plan that includes the cut list optimiser, and every new account starts with a 14-day Pro trial with no card needed. Since I built it, check the others first and see which fits, but the point stands that nobody should be paying just to find out whether optimised nesting helps their shop.

### Does cut list software work with a CNC?

Usually through DXF. The optimiser exports the nested sheet layout as a DXF file, your CAM software such as Vectric or Fusion 360 opens it, and you add toolpaths there. As of July 2026, MaxCut, SmartCUT and OptiCutter export DXF, CutList Plus fx does only in its $499 Platinum edition, and ProCabinet.App exports the nested layout as standard DXF that opens in any CAM software. CutList Optimizer had no DXF export that I could find.

Cabinet Vision goes a step further and drives the machine directly through post-processors, with no separate CAM step. That is a large part of what its price buys, and for most small shops a DXF into the CAM software they already know is plenty.

### Is Cabinet Vision overkill for a small shop?

For a one to three person shop, usually yes. Pricing is quoted per shop rather than published, as of July 2026, the package is modular, and the training time is real. If you mainly need panels nested and a clean cut list, you would be paying for drawing, engineering and machine control you may never use.

The exception is a small shop that already runs a CNC flat out and sells work off proper drawings. At that point Cabinet Vision or something like it stops being overkill and becomes the production system, and the honest comparison is against your machine maker's own software, not against the optimisers in this article.

### Do I really need grain direction and kerf settings?

Yes, and it is not close. Grain direction is the difference between a run of doors that read as one piece of timber and a run that looks patched together. Any optimiser without a grain lock will happily rotate a door 90 degrees to save 2% of a sheet, and the customer will spot it from the doorway.

Kerf is quieter but just as costly. A 3mm blade taken out of every cut adds up across a full sheet, and a layout that ignores it produces parts that are each a touch small or a final panel that no longer fits the board. Both settings take a minute to configure and then you never think about them again, which is exactly how workshop software should behave.
